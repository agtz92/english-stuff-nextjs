mk1: >-
  You open an app, try to make a simple transaction, and suddenly the fee looks
  weirdly aggressive. That moment is exactly why people keep asking, why are gas
  fees high?


  The short answer is traffic. When too many people try to use the same network
  at once, prices jump. But that quick explanation leaves out the part that
  actually matters to regular users: why fees can feel random, why they spike so
  fast, and why one quiet hour can look completely different from the next.
mk2: >-
  ## Why are gas fees high when demand jumps?


  Gas fees are the price users pay to get actions processed on a network. Every
  swap, transfer, mint, or smart contract interaction takes computing work. That
  work is limited, which means network space is limited too.


  When activity stays calm, fees tend to stay manageable. When everyone rushes
  in at once, users start competing for faster confirmation. The result is
  basically a bidding war. The more urgent the demand, the more expensive that
  network space becomes.


  That is why gas fees often spike during major launches, trending tokens,
  high-volume trading periods, and viral online moments. It is not always about
  your transaction being complex. Sometimes it is simply bad timing.
mk3: >-
  ## The network can only handle so much at once


  The biggest driver of high gas fees is capacity. A network can process only a
  certain amount of activity in a given block or time window. If more requests
  come in than the network can comfortably handle, fees rise because not every
  transaction can be included immediately.


  Think of it like surge pricing for a ride app. On a quiet Tuesday afternoon,
  prices are normal. During a rainstorm at rush hour, prices jump because demand
  outruns supply. Gas fees work in a similar way.


  This is also why users sometimes see huge differences between simple and
  advanced actions. Sending funds from one wallet to another may use less
  computational effort than interacting with a busy smart contract. If the
  network is congested and your action is more resource-heavy, the fee can climb
  fast.
mk4: >-
  ## Not all transactions are equal


  One reason gas fees feel confusing is that people assume every action should
  cost about the same. It does not work that way.


  A basic transfer is usually lighter than approving a token, placing a trade,
  listing an item, or interacting with a complex app. Some actions require more
  steps behind the scenes, and more steps mean more gas used. So even if the
  network is only moderately busy, certain transactions can still look
  expensive.


  That is where frustration kicks in. A user sees one fee on a simple action,
  then gets hit with a much bigger number on something that feels similar. From
  the outside, it looks inconsistent. Underneath, the amount of network work is
  different.
mk5: >-
  ## Fee spikes often come from behavior, not just technology


  Here is the part that catches people off guard: high gas fees are not always
  caused by a technical issue. They are often caused by human behavior.


  If a new project launches, people rush in. If [prices move
  sharply](https://www.3minread.com/bitcoin-ethereum-xrp-traders-brace-for-fomc-decision-and-trump%E2%80%99s-crypto-push-%E2%80%93-is-it-time-to-buy),
  traders react fast. If social media hypes something at the same time, a wave
  of users shows up together. Networks do not care why the crowd arrived. They
  just register a surge in demand.


  That means gas fees can become a kind of live mood ring for the internet.
  Excitement, [panic, speculation, and
  hype](https://www.3minread.com/nansen-crypto-eyes-%E2%80%98good-news%E2%80%99-amid-fragile-market-psychology)
  all show up in the fee market. A lot of the time, what you are really seeing
  is crowded behavior translated into numbers.


  ## Why gas fees can stay high even after the rush


  Sometimes the confusing part is not the spike. It is the stubbornness. A user
  waits a bit, comes back later, and fees are still elevated.


  There are a few reasons for that. First, congestion can take time to clear. If
  enough pending transactions stack up, the network may stay busy long after the
  original rush started. Second, people keep bidding high because they do not
  want to wait. That keeps the market heated.


  There is also a psychological effect. Once users see fees rising, many choose
  to overpay just to avoid being stuck. That behavior can help keep average fees
  higher than expected, even when the worst of the traffic has already passed.


  ## Timing matters more than most people think


  If you have ever checked a fee, waited 20 minutes, and watched it drop, that
  was not luck. Timing really does matter.


  Gas fees often shift based on hour, day, market activity, and major online
  events. Busy periods tend to overlap with moments when more US users are
  active, when global traders are reacting to news, or when a popular platform
  is getting hammered by traffic. Late-night and off-peak windows can sometimes
  offer better pricing, though there is no universal rule.


  That is why seasoned users rarely treat one fee quote as permanent. They know
  the same action can cost meaningfully less at another time.


  ## Why are gas fees high on some apps more than others?


  Not every app creates the same amount of demand, and not every action on a
  network competes in the same way. Some platforms attract bursts of activity
  because they are trendy, launch-driven, or heavily traded. Others stay
  relatively calm.


  So if you are wondering why are gas fees high on one app but not another, it
  usually comes down to two things: how complex the action is and how many other
  people are trying to do something similar right now.


  A quiet app doing simple transactions may feel cheap. A high-traffic app with
  lots of contract activity can feel pricey fast. Same broader network, very
  different user experience.


  ## Higher fees are annoying, but they do serve a purpose


  Nobody enjoys paying extra, but high gas fees are not completely pointless.
  They help prioritize which transactions get processed first when demand
  overwhelms capacity.


  Without a fee market, networks could be easier to clog with spam or low-value
  activity. Fees create a cost for using limited resources, which helps sort
  urgent transactions from less urgent ones. The downside is obvious: when
  demand goes wild, regular users can get priced out.


  That trade-off is the real tension. Fees help keep systems functioning, but
  they can also make everyday use feel expensive at the worst possible moment.


  ## What regular users can actually do about it


  You cannot control network demand, but you can make smarter moves around it.


  The easiest fix is patience. If your transaction is not urgent, waiting can
  save money. Another useful habit is checking the estimated fee before
  approving anything, especially if you are interacting with a more complex app.
  Some users also lower their urgency settings, accepting slower confirmation in
  exchange for a lower cost.


  It also helps to understand what action you are taking. A simple transfer and
  a contract interaction are not priced the same. Knowing that ahead of time can
  prevent the kind of sticker shock that makes people think something is broken.


  And sometimes the answer is simply not to force the transaction. If fees look
  absurdly high, it may be better to come back later than pay a premium out of
  impatience.


  ## The real answer to why are gas fees high


  Most of the time, high gas fees are just the cost of limited space meeting
  sudden demand. They rise when too many users want priority at the same time,
  and they get even worse when the activity itself is more complex.


  That can feel unfair when you are only trying to complete one small action.
  But fees are rarely random. Usually, they are reacting to congestion, user
  competition, and the amount of computing work your transaction requires.


  If you treat gas fees like changing traffic instead of a fixed price tag, they
  make a lot more sense. And when the numbers look ridiculous, the best move is
  often the least exciting one: close the tab, wait a bit, and try again when
  the crowd calms down.
